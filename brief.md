# Brief

## Question

Can contemporary authoritarian, plutocratic, and anti-institutional politics be understood as an anticipatory counterrevolution against a future in which AI, OSINT, and computational audit make impunity impossible to maintain ("the last age of impunity")? Under what conditions does machine legibility produce accountability rather than surveillance or new impunity?

## Claim

The strong thesis fails (movements predate AI; motives multicausal). The narrow mechanism survives and is priced: whether a wrongful act meets a consequence is the PRODUCT of an accountability chain (discovery x attribution x publication x prosecution x judgment x enforcement), and a product is bounded by its weakest term. AI raises the evidence stages toward 1 but not the institutional (capturable) stages. Three results: (1) the bottleneck migrates from evidence to adjudication (pre-AI consequence is 7.8% of the institutional ceiling; post-AI it sits exactly at the ceiling); (2) IMPUNITY IS CONSERVED — cheaper evidence would raise consequence ~13x, but rational capture of the new bottleneck (prosecution+judgment) absorbs the entire gain (102%), returning consequence to ~its pre-AI level; (3) exposure decouples from consequence — the gap between what is known and what is sanctioned widens ~15x (the Panama Papers pattern: all known, all acquitted). The same chain run downward harms a subject ~6x (Scott's ambivalence of legibility). The age AI inaugurates is not the last age of impunity but the age of impunity made visible; the binding constraint is courts and enforcement, not models.

## Cornerstone literature

- The accountability-chain / deterrence frame: Becker (1968, the expected-sanction inequality).
- Legibility as politically ambivalent: Scott (1998, Seeing Like a State); the audit society, Power (1997).
- Surveillance / algorithmic power (the chain turned downward): Foucault (1977); Zuboff (2019); Eubanks (2018); O'Neil (2016); Noble (2018); Crawford (2021); Cohen (2019).
- Democratic erosion / capture of conversion institutions: Levitsky and Ziblatt (2018); Arendt (1973).
- Empirical anchors: Mutz (2018, status threat); Alstadsaeter, Johannesen and Zucman (2019, offshore); ICIJ (Panama Papers/FinCEN); V-Dem, World Justice Project, Transparency International indices.
